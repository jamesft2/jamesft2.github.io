MD Nutshell

Molecular dynamics (MD) is a simulation tool frequently used to understand both atomic and soft systems.

The core concept of MD is the following algorithms

  • Calculate the forces felt by each particle on each particle
  • Compute new positions based on the current forces and velocities (see Verlet algorithm)
  • Adjust the velocities such that they obey the thermostat

Ensembles

Ensembles are a way of saying what is controlled in the system. If your simulation is modeling a closed system, you might say you control the number of particles, volume, and temperature (NVT). If your simulation is modeling an open system, you might say you control the number of particles, pressure, and temperature (NPT). There are other elements you might also want to control which give rise to other ensembles (NPH, NVE, etc.)

Thermostats and Barostats

Nose-Hoover = MTTK ==> This is the thermostat I usually use. Constant Pressure ==> Brenderson Barostat

Units

The units of the simulation are based off the arbitrary decisions of the energy scale (set by the pair potential) and the mass of the particles. Different 'force fields' (which describe the interactions between particles), will set different energy scales. Sometimes, these scales correspond to real units (i.e. Martini ) Othertimes, they are arbirtrary and can be reported in reduced units (i.e. Gaussian chain). Then, the effective time and length scales can be calculated by approximated with known properties (i.e. diffusion or chain length)

Particles and Interactions

MD simulations are particle based simulations. This means they are fundementally different than other continuum level simulation tools and DFT. Some Monte Carlo methods and MPCD Solvent methods are also particle based.

Thus, the largest input parameter to an MD simulation is what are the particles and how do they interact with eachother. These interactions are highly dependent on the system being modeled (and the relevant length scale). A set of interactions is refered to as a 'force field'.

There are two main classes of interactions: Specific and Pair Interactions. Pair interactions act between all pairs of particles in the system, typically modeling van der Waals, electrostatics, hard core repulsion, etc. Specific interactions are used to model chemical or physical interactions which exist due to specific mechanisms not modeled at a given resolution. For example, chemical bonds, bond angles, and dihedral angles all represent features which are not modeled by these particles (but do exist for real atoms), just need to be modeled. https://hoomd-blue.readthedocs.io/en/latest/hoomd/md/module-pair.html https://hoomd-blue.readthedocs.io/en/latest/hoomd/md/module-bond.html

Periodic Boundaries

The simulation box has one unique property -- it is periodic in all directions. It can have any shape, but it will always wrap, such that a particle that leaves the right side of the box will re-enter on the left. As a rule of thumb, the box should be larger than 5 times the size of the largest element of the simulation (i.e. 5 times the radius of gyration of a polymer, 5 times the thickness of a membrane, etc). This runs into issues with things like gels which do span the entire box, and the 'Finite Size' of the system should usually be tested.
